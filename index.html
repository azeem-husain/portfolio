Baja SAE Senior Design

FOUNDING MEMBER · DRIVETRAIN LEAD

Founding member of our university's first collegiate Baja SAE team. With no prior team history, existing designs, or institutional knowledge to build on, the first year centered on establishing a baseline vehicle from scratch — researching SAE rules, benchmarking established programs, and making foundational architecture decisions with no precedent to lean on. One of two team members who drove the car at competition.

My Role

  • Selected drivetrain components — CVT and axles — based on the competition's fixed engine output and off-road torque/handling requirements
  • Modeled the full drivetrain layout in SolidWorks within chassis packaging constraints set by the frame subteam
  • Designed mounting structures and brackets for drivetrain components, accounting for vibration and shock loads from off-road terrain
  • Fabricated chassis components: cut metal plate and tubing, welded the frame, CNC-milled mounting brackets
  • One of two team drivers who competed in the vehicle

Outcome

As a first-year program with no existing baseline, competing at all was a stretch goal. The team designed, built, and fielded a complete vehicle that competed and ran under its own power. Reliability issues surfaced during competition — a common outcome for a first-year vehicle from an entirely new team — providing a concrete baseline and a set of lessons for the following year's team.

RC Hovercraft

Built largely from spare parts accumulated from combat-robot builds, inspired by an interest in RC quadcopters and cars. Three versions, each resolving a specific handling or safety issue from the last.

V1

Foam-core poster board hull. One propeller pushing air downward for lift (the air cushion), a second rear-facing propeller providing thrust over a rudder for steering. Motors mounted to quickly fabricated plastic beams, hot-glued to the body. Electronics placed near the front, away from the props, to reduce fire risk from a prop strike. Also prototyped a tarp-material skirt for a more traditional hovercraft look.

Why: Scrap-parts proof of concept. The tarp skirt was removed after testing showed it made the craft noticeably less controllable — the downward prop stayed, the enclosed air cushion didn't.

V2

Upgraded propellers. Hull material switched from poster board to corrugated plastic for durability and lower friction. Electronics relocated to the rear with a makeshift plastic battery cover.

Why: Rear placement was a deliberate weight-balance experiment to improve handling — it worked, but reintroduced prop-strike fire risk that the cover only partly addressed.

V3 (final)

Battery relocated back to the front. Aerodynamic front cover added to enclose the electronics and clean up the look. Larger rudder for more steering authority, improved wire management. Experimental servo-actuated "handbrake" — a small arm with a hot-glue friction pad.

Why: Resolved the V2 fire-risk trade-off for good. The handbrake was a low-cost experiment that turned out to have little practical effect, but cost nothing to leave in.
