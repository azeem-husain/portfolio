Three versions, each resolving a specific handling or safety issue from the last. V1 Foam-core poster board hull. One propeller pushing air downward for lift (the air cushion), a second rear-facing propeller providing thrust over a rudder for steering. Motors mounted to quickly fabricated plastic beams, hot-glued to the body. Electronics placed near the front, away from the props, to reduce fire risk from a prop strike. Also prototyped a tarp-material skirt for a more traditional hovercraft look. Why: Scrap-parts proof of concept. The tarp skirt was removed after testing showed it made the craft noticeably less controllable — the downward prop stayed, the enclosed air cushion didn't. V2 Upgraded propellers. Hull material switched from poster board to corrugated plastic for durability and lower friction. Electronics relocated to the rear with a makeshift plastic battery cover. Why: Rear placement was a deliberate weight-balance experiment to improve handling — it worked, but reintroduced prop-strike fire risk that the cover only partly addressed. V3 (final) Battery relocated back to the front. Aerodynamic front cover added to enclose the electronics and clean up the look. Larger rudder for more steering authority, improved wire management. Experimental servo-actuated "handbrake" — a small arm with a hot-glue friction pad. Why: Resolved the V2 fire-risk trade-off for good.
